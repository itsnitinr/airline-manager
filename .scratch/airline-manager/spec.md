# Airline Manager Product Spec

Status: implementation-planned

## Vision

Build a web-based airline management game focused on realistic airline
operations and economics. A player can establish an airline, manage a fleet,
purchase fuel, create routes, and maintain aircraft. Aircraft types and
locations must correspond to real-world entities. Game state is persisted by
an actual backend and database.

## Confirmed requirements

- Web-based initial release.
- Persistent backend and database.
- Airline creation and management.
- Fleet acquisition and management.
- Fuel purchasing and consumption.
- Route planning and operation.
- Aircraft maintenance.
- Real-world aircraft types and airports.
- Realism is a primary product goal.
- The initial game is single-player.
- Core domain ownership and simulation boundaries must accommodate a future
  shared multiplayer world.
- Game time and scheduled operations continue while the player is offline.
- Elapsed time is determined authoritatively by the backend rather than the
  browser.
- Real-world flight durations run at approximately 1:1 real time.
- The primary audience is aviation-enthusiast management players.
- Beginners receive guidance and sensible defaults; advanced players can expose
  and tune deeper controls backed by the same simulation.
- Real-world operational and geographic facts anchor a plausible contemporary
  simulation.
- Passenger demand, fares, costs, and competition are generated by the game
  rather than synchronized from the live airline market.
- Players create fictional airlines.
- The initial world has global airport coverage curated to commercially
  relevant airports with sufficient operational data.
- The initial fleet catalog contains specific variants of modern commercial
  passenger jets and turboprops, plus supported factory freighters.
- Historic, prototype, military, helicopter, and private-jet operations are
  outside the initial scope.
- Career is the primary mode: the player starts with limited capital and builds
  a viable airline through operational and financial decisions.
- Airlines can become insolvent. The player account persists and can found a
  replacement airline after failure.
- An unrestricted sandbox is deferred.
- Progression has no arbitrary XP or level gates. Access is determined by
  capital, creditworthiness, aircraft and airport compatibility, staffing,
  maintenance capability, safety, reputation, and regulatory standing.
- Players do not choose a permanent airline archetype. The business model
  emerges from network, fleet, cabin, fare, service, baggage, and cargo choices
  and may change over time.
- Players plan recurring weekly timetables and assign specific aircraft through
  rotations rather than manually dispatching every completed flight.
- Schedule validation covers overlap, range, runway compatibility, aircraft
  position, turnaround, airport constraints, and maintenance windows.
- Templates and assisted scheduling reduce beginner friction without changing
  the scheduling rules.
- A new airline selects one principal base and may establish additional
  operating bases as its capability grows.
- Eligible airports can be served through outsourced ground handling and
  pay-per-use access before the airline invests locally.
- Station investments may include gates, lounges, maintenance capability, crew
  bases, and cargo handling, affecting cost, capacity, reliability, and service.
- Airports can impose capacity, curfew, and slot pressure; players do not build
  complete terminals.
- Airport slot ownership, leasing, utilization rules, and filings are deferred.
- Congested airports instead expose time-dependent fees, turnaround effects,
  delay risk, and hard scheduling capacity validated by the backend.
- Each airline has a home jurisdiction.
- Ordinary domestic and international authority is automatic once capability
  requirements are met; bilateral treaty and permit administration is deferred.
- Foreign domestic cabotage is blocked by the contemporary default ruleset.
- Passenger demand is aggregated by origin-destination market, segment, and
  cabin rather than simulated as individual travelers.
- Flight-level bookings accumulate before departure and respond to fare,
  schedule, journey duration, service, and airline reputation.
- Booking allocation supports direct journeys and valid one-stop connections,
  considering total fare, elapsed time, connection risk, service, and schedule
  convenience.
- Journeys with more than one connection are deferred.
- Cargo demand is aggregated by origin-destination market, weight, volume,
  category, and time sensitivity rather than represented as individual parcels.
- Passenger-aircraft belly capacity and dedicated freighters participate in the
  same cargo market.
- General, express, perishable, and special cargo may require different airport
  handling capability and aircraft eligibility.
- Initial competition is aggregated by market as competing capacity, fare
  pressure, and service quality. The game does not operate hidden full-fidelity
  AI airline fleets.
- The competition model must be able to incorporate real airline supply in a
  future multiplayer world.
- Revenue management sells through cabin-level booking classes over time.
- Beginners can use pricing postures and recommendations; advanced players can
  set base fares, bounds, and load-factor or revenue targets.
- Realized fares respond to remaining inventory, booking pace, seasonality,
  schedule quality, and competition.
- Airlines purchase fungible fuel into a global inventory consumed across all
  flight operations, regardless of airport.
- Airport-level storage, supply, and delivery logistics are deliberately
  abstracted to keep fuel management tractable.
- Global fuel storage is capacity-limited and upgradeable. Capacity expansion
  requires investment.
- Maintenance requirements use flight hours, flight cycles, and calendar limits
  appropriate to each aircraft variant.
- Players schedule routine line work and larger maintenance packages, trading
  capacity and downtime against aircraft availability.
- Deferred work degrades condition and dispatch reliability; unscheduled faults
  can delay or ground aircraft.
- Individual spare-part inventory and component-by-component engineering are
  deferred.
- Fatal accidents and passenger deaths are not simulated.
- Safety failures can cause delays, diversions, cancellations, emergency
  repairs, groundings, fines, insurance increases, reputation loss, enhanced
  oversight, and certificate suspension.
- Staff are represented as role-, base-, and qualification-specific workforce
  pools rather than named individuals.
- Pilot type ratings, cabin staffing, maintenance skills, training lead times,
  wages, fatigue limits, and shortages constrain operations.
- Individual employee personalities, résumés, and manual shift rostering are
  deferred.
- Aircraft acquisition includes new orders, operating leases, used purchases,
  and secured loans.
- A new career selects one subsidized operating lease from a curated starter
  list, including familiar narrow-body and lower-risk regional options.
- The founder package may reduce or waive the initial deposit, but recurring
  lease payments remain. The aircraft is not owned and cannot be sold for cash.
- Standardized founder equity funds a reasonable runway for staffing, fuel,
  maintenance, and initial operations.
- A modest optional founding loan supports expansion. Subsequent loan terms
  depend on collateral, profitability, leverage, safety, and repayment history.
- Long-term leases, loans, and similar commitments use gameplay-compressed terms
  measured in real days rather than literal multi-year waits.
- Flight duration, aircraft hours, cycles, and utilization remain tied to real
  operations rather than the compressed contract term.
- Aircraft chronological age advances 1:1 with real calendar time.
- Used aircraft supply pre-existing age and wear; replacement pressure primarily
  comes from hours, cycles, condition, reliability, maintenance, and economics.
- Calendar-driven maintenance uses explicit balanced real-day thresholds.
- Financial transactions post balanced entries to an immutable double-entry
  ledger.
- Default views prioritize cash, obligations, and route/flight profitability;
  advanced views include profit and loss, balance sheet, cash flow, and journal
  detail.
- Accounting covers ownership, leases, loans, depreciation, fuel inventory,
  wages, maintenance, airport costs, revenue, and other simulated obligations.
- The airline selects one reporting currency at founding.
- Transactions retain original currency and the exchange rate used; reports
  convert into the airline's reporting currency.
- Exchange rates update periodically. Active FX trading and currency hedging are
  deferred.
- Players configure supported cabin classes, capacity, and density within
  aircraft-variant limits.
- Configuration affects capacity, payload, comfort, demand, fares, service cost,
  and turnaround.
- Cabin reconfiguration incurs cost and aircraft downtime.
- Individual seat placement and galley or lavatory editing are deferred.
- Weather is generated internally using geographic and seasonal patterns rather
  than synchronized from live providers.
- Weather can affect wind, visibility, airport capacity, block time, fuel burn,
  delays, diversions, and cancellations.
- Forecasts expose uncertainty so players can plan schedule buffers.
- Forecasts warn about projected fuel, cash, qualified workforce, maintenance,
  and other resource shortages.
- Players can configure minimum cash and fuel reserves.
- A hard shortage automatically suspends affected future flights rather than
  permitting unsafe operation or unlimited cascading debt.
- Suspended flights still incur defined refund, cost, and reputation effects,
  but the failure cascade is bounded while the player is offline.
- Launch uses one balanced economic career ruleset.
- Configurable assistance changes recommendations, warnings, and automation but
  does not modify simulation outcomes or economic difficulty.
- Alternate difficulty rulesets and scenarios are deferred until the standard
  career is validated.
- Development proceeds through deployable vertical slices using the final
  backend, database, ledger, worker, and domain boundaries.
- The first playable slice covers account creation, airline founding, one
  founder lease, a curated reference-data subset, one route and recurring
  schedule, bookings, fuel, maintenance, financial settlement, and offline
  flight completion.
- Slice one supports direct passenger demand, aggregate competition, an economy
  cabin, basic workforce capacity, simulated weather, global fuel inventory,
  maintenance, and ledger-backed settlement.
- Connecting itineraries, dedicated cargo, premium cabins, station investment,
  and full workforce depth are explicitly deferred from slice one.
- Slice one targets roughly 250 curated commercial airports distributed across
  the world.
- Slice one targets four aircraft variants covering a turboprop, regional jet,
  and two familiar narrow-bodies.
- Reference-data expansion uses the import, validation, curation, and promotion
  pipeline rather than code changes.
- Players authenticate before founding an airline.
- Slice one supports verified email/password accounts and Google sign-in using
  secure server-managed sessions.
- Password reset and account recovery are required.
- Anonymous persistent careers are deferred; a later non-persistent demo may be
  separate from career ownership.
- The visual direction is a modern airline operations center anchored by the
  route network map.
- Planning screens are information-dense but use strong hierarchy, forecasts,
  charts, and progressive disclosure rather than spreadsheet-only presentation.
- Typography and color are restrained and premium. Status colors communicate
  actionable operating conditions; cartoon and casino aesthetics are excluded.
- Desktop and laptop layouts provide full route, timetable, fleet, and planning
  capability.
- Mobile web supports monitoring, alerts, fuel purchases, simple pricing
  changes, and bounded safe actions.
- Complex rotation editing on mobile and native mobile applications are deferred.
- Operational, financial, and account events create persisted in-game
  notifications.
- Players may enable browser notifications and configure severity and quiet
  hours.
- Email notifications are deferred from slice one.
- Notification delivery is advisory and cannot replace safe suspension or
  authoritative persisted state.
- Slice one includes a protected internal administration surface.
- Administrators can import, validate, compare, and promote airport and aircraft
  data; version derived and balance values; inspect airlines, flights, jobs,
  notifications, and ledger entries; and safely retry failed work.
- Administrative commands are attributed and audited.
- Corrections to settled flights or financial history use explicit adjustments,
  never silent mutation.
- Broader cargo, connections, station investment, and workforce depth may enter
  later slices rather than blocking the first end-to-end career.
- Realism values are classified as sourced facts, derived simulation values, or
  balance values.
- Sourced facts retain provenance and effective dates. Derived and balance
  values retain formula or ruleset versions.
- Player-facing copy must not present an estimate as a certified manufacturer or
  regulator figure.
- Initial reference data comes from sources permitting redistribution, combined
  with validation and manual curation.
- Candidate sources include public-domain OurAirports data, the IANA timezone
  database, manufacturer publications, and regulator certification documents.
- Imported records retain source, version, retrieval time, attribution, and
  validation status. Raw import does not imply gameplay eligibility.
- Proprietary aviation databases are not scraped or made a required dependency.
- The implementation uses a TypeScript monorepo.
- Next.js provides the web client.
- A separate TypeScript API owns backend commands and queries.
- The API uses Fastify for HTTP routing, schema validation, serialization, and
  OpenAPI integration.
- PostgreSQL is the authoritative persistent database.
- Database access uses Kysely with database-generated TypeScript types.
- Schema changes use explicit, reviewed SQL migrations so PostgreSQL-native
  constraints, indexes, locking, and reporting features remain available.
- Docker Compose runs web, API, worker, PostgreSQL, and Redis for local
  development.
- API and workers ship as production-ready containers; Next.js deploys
  independently.
- Production uses managed PostgreSQL and Redis where appropriate.
- Hosting provider selection is deferred until launch budget and operating needs
  are known.
- Serverless-only deployment is excluded because persistent background workers
  require stable execution.
- Redis-backed job processing coordinates scheduled flights and offline work.
- BullMQ implements delayed work, retries, backoff, deduplication, stalled-job
  handling, and recurring reconciliation.
- Queue payloads contain entity identifiers and expected versions, not
  authoritative financial or simulation state.
- Unit tests use injected time and seeded randomness.
- Property-based tests cover ledger balance, non-negative constrained resources,
  aircraft-position continuity, non-overlapping rotations, and other invariants.
- Integration tests run against real PostgreSQL and Redis services.
- Browser end-to-end tests cover account creation through offline flight
  completion and settlement.
- Replay tests prove that duplicate, stale, and delayed jobs cannot alter a
  settled outcome.
- Fixed balance scenarios assert expected profitability and resource ranges.
- Structured logs correlate API commands, database transitions, outbox records,
  queue jobs, and worker execution.
- Metrics cover job lag, reconciliation backlog, failed transitions,
  suspensions, ledger-posting failures, and SSE health.
- API and workers expose health and readiness checks; errors retain source-map
  context.
- Admin diagnostics expose simulation and job state without requiring direct
  database access.
- Logs exclude passwords, provider tokens, session secrets, and unnecessary
  personal data.
- Simulation workers run separately from HTTP request handling.
- Shared packages contain API contracts, validation, and deterministic domain
  logic.
- Client/backend communication uses versioned REST endpoints documented by
  OpenAPI.
- Mutations are explicit domain commands; queries may use optimized read models.
- Server-Sent Events deliver live flight, maintenance, delivery, and
  notification updates.
- SSE reconnects recover from persisted state; GraphQL is deferred.
- Backend code is organized as a modular monolith with explicit boundaries for
  identity, airlines, fleet, scheduling, markets, operations, maintenance,
  workforce, fuel, and finance.
- API and worker runtimes use the same domain modules and database while
  remaining separately deployable processes.
- Microservice extraction is deferred until concrete scaling or ownership needs
  justify it.
- Normalized PostgreSQL tables store current authoritative game state.
- Important state changes write domain events to a transactional outbox in the
  same commit for asynchronous jobs, notifications, and read models.
- The accounting ledger remains immutable, and settled flights retain immutable
  input/result snapshots.
- Full event sourcing and whole-airline replay are not used initially.
- Queue discrete simulation milestones such as departure, arrival, delivery,
  maintenance completion, and contract payment.
- Periodic reconciliation safely processes overdue or missing milestones.
- Continuous values such as booking accumulation derive from elapsed time and a
  stored checkpoint rather than per-second ticking.
- Every transition is idempotent and safe under duplicate or delayed delivery.
- Availability is dynamic; used aircraft retain age, condition, hours, cycles,
  and maintenance status.
- Delivery delays are gameplay-compressed. Small aircraft may arrive instantly,
  and the maximum delivery wait is 24 real hours.
- Default acquisition channels follow real-world production status:
  discontinued variants are lease/used only. Production status and channel
  eligibility are versioned data so future rules can change them.
- Alternate or future world rulesets may explicitly override acquisition
  eligibility while preserving the underlying real-world production facts and
  existing-career behavior.

## Decision log

- The discovery interview asks and resolves one decision at a time.
- No game implementation begins until the product model reaches shared
  understanding and the user explicitly confirms it.
- The first release is single-player, with multiplayer accounted for in the
  core domain model.
- The simulation uses persistent offline progression governed by the server.
- Flight durations use real elapsed time rather than accelerated global time.
- The game targets enthusiasts through layered complexity and progressive
  disclosure rather than separate simplified and expert simulations.
- Realism is defined as real reference facts plus plausible generated market
  behavior, not a live commercial-aviation replica.
- Airport coverage is worldwide but limited by explicit commercial relevance
  and reference-data quality rules.
- Aircraft are represented as curated modern commercial variants rather than
  generic families or an exhaustive aviation catalog.
- Career mode with real economic constraints is the primary progression model.
- Airline capability, not player level, gates advanced operations.
- Business models emerge from operating choices rather than fixed classes.
- Recurring timetables and aircraft rotations are the core scheduling model.
- Airport presence ranges from outsourced service to invested stations and
  operating bases.
- Airport congestion matters without requiring player-managed slot rights.
- Major traffic-rights boundaries are enforced without a permit-management game.
- Passenger behavior uses segmented aggregate demand and flight-level bookings.
- Network demand recognizes direct and one-stop itineraries.
- Cargo has category-aware aggregate demand constrained by weight and volume.
- Single-player competitors are aggregate commercial pressure, not complete AI
  companies.
- Pricing combines player strategy with automated booking-class management.
- Fuel purchasing uses global airline inventory rather than airport-local stock.
- Fuel storage capacity is a constrained, expandable airline asset.
- Maintenance models planned utilization-based work and unscheduled faults
  without component-level parts logistics.
- Safety has serious non-fatal operational and regulatory consequences; fatal
  disaster is outside the simulation.
- Staffing is capacity-based and qualification-aware, not person-by-person.
- Fleet acquisition preserves meaningful market and financing choices while
  capping delivery time at 24 hours for player engagement.
- Careers begin with a strategic starter-aircraft choice, not a free owned asset.
- Initial working capital is equity with optional debt; ongoing access to debt
  is capability-based.
- Strategic contract durations are compressed without globally accelerating
  operational time.
- Aircraft chronological age remains on real calendar time.
- Financial reports and balances derive from a proper accounting ledger.
- Global operations use multi-currency transactions and one reporting currency.
- Cabin strategy is configurable at class and density level, not seat-map level.
- Operational weather is plausible and simulated, not live.
- Offline progression has real consequences with automatic safe suspension.
- Player assistance is configurable; economic rules are singular at launch.
- Product breadth is phased through real end-to-end vertical slices.
- Slice one proves the direct economy-passenger flight lifecycle.
- Slice-one reference breadth is approximately 250 airports and four aircraft
  variants.
- Persistent careers require authenticated accounts.
- The product presents a premium, map-led airline operations interface.
- The web app is desktop-first for planning and mobile-capable for monitoring.
- Slice-one notifications are in-app and optionally browser-delivered; email is
  deferred.
- Protected, audited administration is a slice-one requirement.
- Data provenance distinguishes real facts from modeled and balanced values.
- Open data is curated into a versioned playable catalog.
- Web, API, and worker applications are separate TypeScript deployments sharing
  domain packages.
- REST/OpenAPI is the primary client contract, supplemented by recoverable SSE.
- Backend domain boundaries are modular without adopting microservices initially.
- Fastify is a thin API adapter; workers do not depend on the HTTP framework.
- Kysely is the typed SQL layer; PostgreSQL and explicit migrations own schema.
- Deployment is containerized and provider-neutral.
- State plus an outbox provides auditability and delivery without full event sourcing.
- Simulation advances through milestones and reconciliation, not global ticks.
- BullMQ transports simulation work; PostgreSQL determines eligibility and outcome.
- Deterministic and invariant-driven verification is required for simulation work.
- Slice one includes an operational observability baseline.
- Discontinued aircraft are not factory-new in the contemporary default, but
  acquisition eligibility is configurable rather than hard-coded.
- Non-contemporary acquisition rules are explicit, versioned world settings.
